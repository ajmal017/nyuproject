What is the difference between saving and investing?

While they may seem similar, saving and investing actually are very different in nature and have different goals.

Savings is putting money aside for future use. The purpose of having savings is so you can pay for things that you need, for example your monthly mortgage payment. In addition, it also helps you to prepare for emergencies. For example, in case you become unemployed, you will be drawing upon the money available in your savings account whilst you look for another job or source of income. Your savings money is generally put into bank accounts where it is insured by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) for an amount up to $250,000. This means that there is virtually no risk

Investing is when you put your money to work. The purpose of an investment is to increase the value of your money over time. For example, if you invested in the stock of Apple, you are thinking that the value of the company Apple will also increase over time. The benefit of investing is that there is potential for greater returns than if you had put the money into a bank account. However, your investments are not insured by any organization and there is often a risk associated with investing.


Why should I invest?

Investing can help you achieve financial goals, like buying a home or funding your investment. By investing, you are putting your money to work to achieve these goals.

One important concept is the power of compounding. This is the idea of making returns on returns. This is when your returns grow by a larger and larger amount. The image below gives you a visual representation of the power of compounding.

How much should I invest?

Every person is different and has different investment needs. There is no one size fits all solution and you should assess your personal needs. Some factors to consider are below:

  • How much you have - Can you put aside enough money for an emergency fund, pay rent, pay other daily living expenses etc.?
  • Time - What is your investment horizon, or for how long would you be able to save the money?
  • Goal - What are you saving for?
  • Risk - What is the degree of uncertainty or how much potential financial loss am I able to incur?

What are the downsides to investing?

Investing has risks. This is one of the factors that you should be considering when deciding whether to invest. However, this does not mean that you shouldn’t invest at all. Rather, it is important to manage the risks associated with investing.

One way to manage risk is through diversification. This means investing in different things such that you don’t have all of your eggs in one basket. For example, you may want to put your money in both Apple stock and Disney stock, instead of just one. Or, you may want to divide your money among different types of investments, such as stocks and bonds. Additionally, different types of investments perform differently. For example, during the financial crisis, the prices of equities decreased significantly, whilst the prices of fixed income securities remained relatively stable.


You mentioned common types of investments above, so what are some common types of investments?

There are several main types of investments:

  1. Stocks which represent ownership in a company. You can get return from investing in a stock either by (1) Buying the stock and as the stock increases, you can sell it at a profit or (2) Buying the stock and receive dividend distributions which companies give to shareholders.
    • The risk level for investing in stocks is high as stock price may decrease and there is no guarantee of dividends
  2. Bonds which represent a loan that you make to the issuer, which can be the federal government, the state government, the municipality, or a corporation. In return, the issuer agrees to pay you interest periodically and repay you the initial value of the loan after a certain period of time. Bonds can provide a predictable stream of cash flows and they also preserve your initial capital, since you are paid the initial value of the loan when the bond matures.
    • There is a risk level in investing in a bond, as the issuer could default on its loan or pay back the loan before it matures. However, bonds are generally less volatile than stocks.
  3. Mutual Funds which pools money from investors and invests them in a broad range of investments including stocks, bonds, and other securities. After you buy into a mutual fund, the fund is managed by an investment advisor. You will have a stake on all the investments that the fund invests in.
  4. Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) pool money from investors and usually tracks an underlying index. In other words, ETFs replicates the performance of the underlying index, for example the S&P 500. ETFs are useful because it may be hard for individual investors to track an underlying index otherwise. ETFs allow for electronic trading, give you the ability to use leverage to generate more returns.
  5. Annuities are a form of investment where the company gives a stream of income payments to the purchaser. Specifically, the income payment continues until the user dies. In other words, the user cannot outlive the income stream. After the purchaser submits a payment, the insurance company invests the payment into options or bonds to generate a future flow of income which they then pay to the users.
